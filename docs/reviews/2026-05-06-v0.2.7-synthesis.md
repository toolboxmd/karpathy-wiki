## Meta-pattern (both reviewers, independent paths)

Opus: *"prose is the load-bearing enforcement mechanism for at least 8
contracts."*

Codex (adversarial): *"large prose chunks are not actionable in shell
or Python"; "evidence mapping over-specified as prose defaults rather
than enforced contract."*

The 4-skill split made the prose-vs-code gap **worse**, not better.
When contracts were in one 234-line monolith, drift was easier to
spot. Now contracts span three skills + references + scripts, and
the only enforcement layer is the LLM reading prose.

## Closing observation

Of 34 items raised across three adversarial passes, **12 ship in 0.2.8**.
Of the remaining 22:

- **3** deferred with concrete next-trigger criteria.
- **6** deferred with revisit-when criteria (mostly "revisit when this
  actually fires").
- **11** rejected as phantom contracts or unobserved-failure defenses
  (down from 13 after #3 and #13 were promoted on the LLM-substrate
  argument).

This pattern is healthy: adversarial reviews surface lots of *could
break* paths; the project's job is to know which actually do break,
versus which are reviewers being thorough. The v1-monolith reviewer
contract said "no vector search until genuine scaling pain" — same
principle applied to defensive features.

The single biggest unfixed structural issue (per Opus) — *prose as
load-bearing enforcement* — is not addressed in 0.2.8 but is logged.
Item-by-item migration to code-enforced contracts will happen
incrementally as each prose contract demonstrably rots.
